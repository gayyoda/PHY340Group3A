Mission Equipment

If a long-stay, semi-direct method is adopted, there are many stages of the mission to consider.

Pre-Crew Launch

Large quantities of equipment need to be fully developed years before the crew launch. The key components are:

  • Mars Ascent Vehicle (MAV) + power station to fuel it
  • Mars Surface Habitat (SHAB)
  • Exploration Rover
  • Mars Communication and Observation Satellite (MCOS)
  • Heliocentric Laser Communications Relay Satellite (HLCRS)

The MAV, SHAB and Rover need to be sent directly to the Martian surface, whereas the two statellies will remain in Mars orbit. This all needs to be sent before the crew launch. Super heavy rockets will transfer this equipment directly from Earth to Mars. If the goal is to get boots on Mars by 2040, all this needs to be developed now so that they are ready well in advance of the launch [33].

Crewed Launch

As discussed previously, launching the Mars transfer vehicle (MTV) from a low Earth orbit (LEO) is very beneficial. As such, the MTV will need to be sent into a low Earth orbit and be fuelled in situ. This will require multiple rocket launches in order to completely fuel the MTV. Once fueled, the crew will then need to rendezvous with the MTV in orbit, where they can then begin the journey to Mars. Once they reach Mars orbit, they transfer from the MTV into a capsule which can take them down to the surface [33]. As mentioned in section 4.3, low-thrust propulsion technologies are recommended for uncrewed establishment of cycler obits from LEO.

Again, in order to get to Mars by 2040 then the fueling process logistics and development of the MAV need to start now, especially since it can take up to 6 years to fully establish the LEO. All this makes the goal of 2040 uncertain.

This information is from the online version of Shaun Moss’s book, titled ‘The International Mars Research Station’. The book is a very realistic depiction of a possible Mars mission, and is extensively researched. As such, it is a reliable source of information [33].
