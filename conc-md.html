Conclusion: Mission Design

The safest and most efficient way to reach Mars is on a semi direct, conjunction class trajectory. This minimises medical risks as it reduces the time spent in zero gravity and allows for significant transit recovery time on the surface. Moreover it allows for preferable alignment of Mars and Earth for the return journey, enabling Aldrinâ€™s cycler orbit to be utilised.

The crew would spend approximately 550 days on the surface, meaning additional equipment is required for a long stay (such as the SHAB). While this increases the cost and logistics, it allows for a much better return on investment due to the lengthy surface stay time. Additionally, there is potential to share manufacturing costs with other nations who would use the same equipment.

The crew will eventually leave Mars in a Mars Ascent Vehicle (MAV). This is sent to the surface before the crew, along with other equipment, and is fuelled on Mars. At the end of the mission, the MAV launches from the surface and rendezvouses with an Earth Return Vehicle (ERV) in orbit. To effectively achieve all this, the transport vehicles need to be fuelled in a low Earth orbit, which needs to be established years before the mission.
