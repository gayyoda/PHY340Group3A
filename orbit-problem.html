Cycler Orbits Problems

Two major issues with manned Mars missions are how we resupply in-progress missions and how we get there and back in the first place. It is possible to get there by waiting for an appropriate transfer window and use a simple approach, but a more economical approach could be to use a cycler orbit between Earth and Mars, as this can greatly reduce both the fuel cost and the per-launch cost.

A cycler is an orbit that repeatedly encounters two objects, in this case Earth and Mars. Cycler orbits were first described by Hollister and Menning in the 1960s [21-24] for the case of ballistic trajectories between Earth and Venus. Later, Rall and Hollister showed that there also exist such trajectories between Earth and Mars [25]. In the 1980s, Aldrin also described an Earth-Mars cycler [26]. Given Aldrin’s history, it is fair to say that his work will be fairly accurate and reliable. In addition, there have been later analyses of this work, [27] for example, that have been published in peer-reviewed journals.

A method for constructing such orbits [27] was published in Acta Astronautica, which is a peer-reviewed journal sponsored by the International Academy of Astronautics, and an analysis of several of these noted possible ways of saving on fuel while placing spacecraft into them. Based on Aldrin’s cycler, there is an opportunity for a 5-month Earth-Mars trip and a 4-month Mars-Earth trip every synodic period, [26] which is the time required for an object to return to the same point relative to two or more other objects.
